Connection

On occasion, we may get more than what we ask for.In the case of Anita Hajivandi, shemakes an effort to provide more than just the packagesof food for those visiting the food pantryshe co-directs with her husband, Moe, from the Riverof Life church in southeastern Rutland,Ohio, just a few miles from the Ohio River borderingWest Virginia. There’s a reassurance thatbeyond the food that helps visitors get through eachweek, there’s a connection that some may belonging for. Hajivandi is certain that, with food,there is always that bond you form with oneanother. No matter how the food is prepared or howit’s received, it’s a gift that comes with nostrings attached -- just the natural act of givingand receiving.

A native of the area in the foothills of Appalachia,Hajivandi is all too familiar with theregion she grew up in. “I grew up on a great farm.. . a hundred acres. We ran wild. . . I had agreat life,” she says, reminiscing on her adolescentyears. “I really embrace it. I think it was awonderful childhood. It was a childhood of work. .. you work hard, you play hard.”

Amid fond memories of picking blackberries in thesummertime and adjusting to life atOhio University, Anita fell into the culinary arts-- something that combined her creative andmechanical spirit. She may have the ability to executeany dish from any cultural origin fromaround the globe, but it’s the food she grew up within Appalachia that stuck to her ribs the most.The typical breakfast prepared for her while growingup was coffee-fried pork with cured ham,sausage gravy biscuits, fried apples and eggs witha cup of coffee that would be poured into thesaucer, then back into the cup, proving an effectiveway to cool the piping hot beverage. Thiswas a staple meal made for coal miners, accordingto Hajivandi. They needed a big, fillingbreakfast before indulging in a day of hard work,but the same goes for school children andanyone who was in need of a full stomach. The foodprepared was in accordance with thesurrounding environment, like every staple dish iscreated. Growing up with a single mother and her grandparents, there was no influx of money pouring into her life. Going out to restaurantswas never an option while living in rural Appalachia.The food she ate, like many otherAppalachians, was harvested in the summer and preservedfor the fall and winter. It was thestandard. The change of seasons controlled what wason the plate and with these memories oflife on the farm draws a greater significance to thecuisine that Hajivandi still prepares today.One’s senses instantly take them back to a memorywhen it’s triggered by that one thing, and it’sthe smell of pork and sausage, the look of the sorghummolasses combined with butter and thetaste of it all that allows Anita to see her grandmotherin the food she cooks. With the food weprepare and eat, it’s a gateway to an open table.Perhaps this is why Hajivandi dedicates her timewith the food pantry. Before the coronavirus alteredlife as we knew it, her culinary skills werebrought to the kitchen to prepare meals for visitorsto enjoy and open the doors to the church foreveryone to come in for a feast. Bringing togetherthe community with food is one thing, but tocook the food with love in this sense carries a meaninggreater than anything else. It’s cookingwith intention that creates the connection.
